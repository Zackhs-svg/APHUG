Unit 1: Thinking Geographically

Absolute Location: The exact position of a place on the Earth's surface, often given in latitude and longitude.
Relative Location: The position of a place in relation to another place.
Map Projection: A method for representing the curved surface of the Earth on a flat map, with varying degrees of distortion.
Spatial Patterns: Describes where phenomena are located on Earth and how they are arranged, such as clustering or dispersal.
Clustering: A spatial pattern where phenomena are grouped closely together.
Dispersal: A spatial pattern where phenomena are spread apart with more space between them.
Elevation: The height of a geographical location relative to sea level, often depicted on isoline maps.
Scale: The relationship between distance on a map and the actual distance on Earth. Can be represented as a ratio or bar scale.
Large-Scale Map: A map that shows a smaller area with greater detail.
Small-Scale Map: A map that shows a larger area with less detail.
Choropleth Map: A thematic map that uses different colors to represent data density or values across regions.
Dot Distribution Map: A thematic map using dots to visualize the location or density of phenomena.
Graduated Symbol Map: A thematic map using symbols of varying sizes to indicate the magnitude of phenomena.
Isoline Map: A thematic map using lines to connect points of equal value, commonly used for elevation or weather data.
Cartogram: A thematic map that distorts the size of regions based on data values, such as population.
Mercator Projection: A map projection that preserves direction but distorts sizes, especially near the poles.
Peters Projection: A map projection that preserves area size but distorts shapes.
Robinson Projection: A compromise map projection that balances size and shape distortions across the map.
Geographic Information System (GIS): Software for analyzing and visualizing spatial data, used for research or decision-making.
Global Positioning System (GPS): A satellite-based system used to determine the precise location of an object or place on Earth.
Remote Sensing: The process of gathering data about Earth's surface from satellites or aerial imagery.

Unit 2: Population and Migration

Population Distribution: The pattern of where people live, influenced by physical and human factors.
Population Density: A measure of how many people occupy a given unit of land.
Arithmetic Density: Total population divided by total land area.
Physiological Density: Total population divided by the amount of arable land.
Agricultural Density: Number of farmers divided by the amount of arable land.
Dependency Ratio: Ratio of dependents (under 15, over 65) to the working-age population.
Sex Ratio: Number of males compared to females in a population.
Population Pyramid: A graph showing age and sex structure of a population.
Crude Birth Rate (CBR): Births per 1,000 people per year.
Total Fertility Rate (TFR): Average number of children a woman has in her lifetime.
Crude Death Rate (CDR): Deaths per 1,000 people per year.
Rate of Natural Increase (RNI): CBR - CDR, indicating population growth or decline without migration.
Doubling Time: Time it takes for a population to double at current growth rates.
Carrying Capacity: Maximum population an environment can sustain.
Demographic Transition Model (DTM): Shows how population changes as a society industrializes.
Epidemiological Transition Model (ETM): Explains changing death rates due to disease patterns.
Malthusian Theory: Idea that population growth could outpace food production.
Pro-Natalist Policies: Policies encouraging higher birth rates.
Anti-Natalist Policies: Policies aimed at reducing birth rates.
Push Factors: Negative conditions prompting people to leave a location.
Pull Factors: Positive conditions attracting people to a new location.
Intervening Obstacles: Factors that impede migration to a certain destination.
Intervening Opportunities: Factors that cause migrants to settle before reaching their original destination.
Forced Migration: Migration compelled by external factors (e.g., war, persecution).
Voluntary Migration: Migration by choice, often for economic or social reasons.

Unit 3: Cultural Patterns and Processes

Culture: Shared practices, technologies, attitudes, and behaviors transmitted by a society.
Cultural Traits: Visible and invisible attributes that combine to form a groupâ€™s culture, such as language, clothing, architecture, and land use.
Culture Iceberg Model: A way to visualize culture in which observable cultural traits are above the surface, while underlying values, beliefs, and thought patterns lie below.
Food Preferences: Culinary traditions and tastes that are influenced by cultural history and geography.
Architecture (Cultural): The style and design of buildings and structures that reflect the cultural values, beliefs, and resources of the people who build them.
Land Use: The ways in which a culture utilizes the environment, including agricultural practices, residential patterns, and urban design, reflecting cultural values.
Cultural Relativism: The evaluation of a culture by its own standards, seeking to understand a culture on its own terms without judgment.
Ethnocentrism: Judging another culture by the standards of oneâ€™s own culture, often leading to the perception that oneâ€™s own culture is superior.
Xenophobia: A fear or dislike of foreigners or strangers, often arising from ethnocentric views.
Cultural Landscape: The visible imprint of human activity and culture on the landscape, including built environments, agricultural systems, signs, and sacred sites.
Agricultural and Industrial Practices: Ways societies produce goods and food that shape the cultural landscape, from local farming techniques to globalized industrial activities.
Sacred Spaces: Areas within the cultural landscape that hold religious or spiritual significance, such as temples, churches, mosques, or synagogues.
Linguistic Characteristics: Language-related features of a cultural landscape, such as multilingual signs or place names that reflect cultural identity.
Sequent Occupance: The idea that successive societies leave their cultural imprints on a place, contributing to the cumulative cultural landscape.
Traditional Architecture: Building styles that use locally available materials, reflecting local traditions, environment, and cultural values.
Postmodern Architecture: A style of architecture that arose as a reaction against modernism, often emphasizing form, individuality, and cultural expression.
Ethnicity: A groupâ€™s cultural identity based on shared traits such as language, religion, traditions, and ancestral origins.
Gender and the Cultural Landscape: How cultural attitudes about gender roles and norms influence the way spaces and places are organized or used.
Ethnic Enclaves: Clusters of people of the same culture, often surrounded by a different cultural group, shaping the local cultural landscape.
Indigenous Communities: Groups native to a region who maintain traditional cultures and often have autonomy in designated territories or reservations.
Placemaking: The process by which people create, shape, and give meaning to places, reflecting cultural values and identities.
Sense of Place: The feelings and attitudes people have about a location, shaped by personal experiences, cultural attachments, and historical meaning.
Dialect: A regional variation of a language, characterized by distinct vocabulary, spelling, and pronunciation.
Centripetal Forces: Cultural characteristics or events that unify a society and strengthen the state, such as a shared religion or language.
Centrifugal Forces: Cultural characteristics or events that divide a society, creating instability or tension, such as differing religious sects or ethnic conflicts.
Cultural Diffusion: The spread of cultural traits from a cultural hearth to other areas, often resulting in changes to both cultures.
Relocation Diffusion: The spread of cultural traits through the physical movement of people from one place to another.
Expansion Diffusion: The spread of cultural traits outward from the hearth while the people or communities of the hearth remain in place.
Contagious Diffusion: A form of expansion diffusion where a cultural trait spreads rapidly, widely, and continuously to adjacent regions.
Hierarchical Diffusion: A form of expansion diffusion in which a cultural trait spreads from people or nodes of authority or influence down to others.
Stimulus Diffusion: A form of expansion diffusion in which a cultural trait inspires a new innovation that retains some original elements but also transforms them.
Lingua Franca: A language used among speakers of different native languages for the purposes of trade and communication.
Creolization: The blending of two or more languages or cultures into a new, distinct cultural form or language (Creole languages).
Globalization: The increasing interconnectedness of the worldâ€™s economies, societies, and cultures, facilitating more rapid cultural exchange.
Urbanization: The movement of people from rural areas to cities, influencing cultural diffusion as diverse groups interact in urban environments.
Time-Space Convergence: The idea that distant places are brought closer together in terms of time taken to travel, communicate, and interact due to technological advancements.
Cultural Convergence: When cultures become more similar due to frequent interactions and the widespread adoption of shared traits.
Cultural Divergence: When a culture restricts outside influences and maintains distinct traditions, resisting cultural diffusion and assimilation.
Universalizing Religions: Religions that seek to unite people from a wide variety of cultural backgrounds, often diffusing widely through various types of expansion and relocation diffusion.
Ethnic Religions: Religions closely tied to a particular ethnic group or location, generally not seeking new converts, and thus remaining more localized.
Toponyms: Place names that can reflect cultural diffusion, heritage, and changes in power or religion over time.
Acculturation: The process in which individuals or groups adopt some traits of another culture while retaining aspects of their own cultural identity.
Assimilation: The process by which a person or groupâ€™s culture comes to resemble those of another group, often resulting in the loss of the minority groupâ€™s original cultural identity.
Syncretism: The blending of two or more cultural traits or beliefs to form a new, distinct cultural trait or practice.
Multiculturalism: The coexistence of diverse cultures in the same space, allowing each culture to maintain distinct cultural traits while interacting with others.

Unit 4: Political Patterns and Processes

Sovereignty: The authority of a state to govern itself without external interference.
Nation-State: A state whose territory corresponds to a single cultural group that has become a nationality.
Centrifugal Forces: Factors that divide or tend to pull a state apart, such as internal conflicts or social divisions.
Centripetal Forces: Factors that unite or bind a state together, such as a strong national identity or shared culture.
Boundary Types: Different kinds of borders, including geometric, physical, cultural, and superimposed boundaries.
Federal State: A state with a division of power between a central government and regional governments.
Unitary State: A state governed as a single entity with a central authority holding most of the power.
Devolution: The transfer of political power from the central government to lower levels of government, often in response to regional demands.
Gerrymandering: The manipulation of electoral district boundaries to favor one party or group.
State: A politically organized territory with a permanent population, defined boundaries, and a government with sovereignty.
Nation: A group of people with a shared cultural heritage, history, and often a common language or religion, seeking political identity.
Stateless Nation: A cultural group that does not have its own independent state or sovereignty over its territory.
Multinational State: A country that contains more than one nation within its borders.
Multistate Nation: A nation that stretches across borders and across states.
Autonomous Region: An area within a state that has a high degree of self-government and freedom from the central authority.
Semiautonomous Region: A region that has some degree of self-rule but is not completely independent.
Self-Determination: The right of a people to determine their own political status and form their own allegiances and government.
Irredentism: A movement to unite people who share a cultural or ethnic background but are divided by a national boundary.
Colonialism: The practice of one country establishing settlements and imposing its political and economic control in another territory.
Imperialism: The broader policy of extending a country's influence and power through diplomacy, military force, or economic dominance.
Independence Movements: Efforts by groups of people to gain political autonomy or full sovereignty over their territory.
Genocide: The intentional attempt to destroy, in whole or in part, a national, ethnic, racial, or religious group.
Ethnic Cleansing: The forced removal or elimination of an ethnic group from a particular area to create a more homogeneous region.
Nationalism: A strong feeling of pride in and devotion to oneâ€™s nation, often promoting its interests above others.
Regionalism: Loyalty to a distinct region with a population that shares similarities, potentially stronger than loyalty to a state as a whole.
Ethnonationalism: Support for the interests of a particular ethnic group, often seeking greater autonomy or independence.
Balkanization: The fragmentation of a state into smaller, often hostile units along ethno-linguistic lines.
Shatter belt: A region caught between stronger, conflicting external forces, under persistent stress, and often fragmented by aggressive rivals.
Globalization: The increasing interconnectedness of the worldâ€™s economies, societies, and cultures through networks of communication and trade.
Supranationalism: The process of states organizing into one or more international institutions or organizations that influence and limit their sovereignty.
Democratization: The transition from autocratic forms of government to more representative and democratic systems.
Transnational Corporations: Companies that conduct business on a global scale and are not strongly connected to any single country.
Time-Space Compression: The reduction in the time it takes for information, people, and goods to travel, due to improvements in transportation and communication.
Choke Points: Narrow passages, such as straits or canals, through which transportation and trade must pass, often strategically important.
Territoriality: The attempt to influence or control people and events by asserting control over a geographic area.
Neocolonialism: The indirect economic, political, or cultural influence exerted by developed states on less developed states, often a legacy of colonialism.
Boundary: An invisible line that marks the extent of a stateâ€™s territory and its control.
Defined Boundary: A boundary established by a legal document or treaty to separate political entities.
Delimited Boundary: A boundary drawn on a map to show the limits of a space.
Demarcated Boundary: A boundary identified by physical objects placed on the landscape, such as a fence, wall, or sign.
Antecedent Boundary: A boundary drawn before a large population was present, often based on physical features.
Subsequent Boundary (Ethnographic Boundary): A boundary drawn after the development of the cultural landscape, to accommodate religious, ethnic, or linguistic differences.
Superimposed Boundary: A boundary drawn by outside powers, often ignoring existing cultural patterns.
Relic Boundary: A boundary that no longer exists as an international border but remnants of its existence remain on the landscape.
Geometric Boundary: A straight line or arc drawn by people that does not closely follow any physical feature.
Consequent Boundary: A type of subsequent boundary that takes into account cultural or physical differences when drawn.
Cultural Consequent Boundary: A boundary that aligns with differences in cultural traits such as religion or language.
Physical Consequent Boundary: A boundary that aligns with natural features in the landscape, such as mountains or rivers.
Open Boundary: A boundary where crossing is unimpeded, often allowing free movement of people and goods.
Militarized Boundary: A heavily guarded boundary that discourages crossing and movement through use of military presence or barriers.
Administered Boundary: A boundary that is managed, maintained, and regulated by a government or legal entity.
Controlled Border: A boundary where a country regulates and monitors the entry of people and goods, often requiring passports or visas.
Territorial Sea: The area of sea extending up to 12 nautical miles from a stateâ€™s coastline, over which a state has sovereignty.
Contiguous Zone: A maritime zone adjacent to a territorial sea, up to 24 nautical miles, where a state can enforce laws on customs, immigration, and sanitation.
Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ): Up to 200 nautical miles from a stateâ€™s shore, where the state has the right to explore, exploit, and manage natural resources.
High Seas: Areas of the ocean beyond the EEZ, open to all states and not under any single stateâ€™s jurisdiction.
Reapportionment: The redistribution of legislative seats according to population changes, often after a census.
Redistricting: The redrawing of internal political boundaries, usually to ensure equal population in electoral districts.
Cracking: A form of gerrymandering where a concentrated group of voters is split into several districts to dilute their influence.
Packing: A gerrymandering technique where like-minded voters are concentrated in one district to reduce their influence in other districts.
Stacking: A gerrymandering strategy that merges minority populations with majority populations to reduce minority representation.
Hijacking: A gerrymandering tactic that forces two incumbents of the same party to run against each other, ensuring one will lose.
Kidnapping: A gerrymandering method where a district is redrawn so that an incumbentâ€™s supporters are placed in a district in which they have less influence.
Annexation: Legally adding territory to a city or state.
Sub nationalism: When people give their primary allegiance to a traditional group or ethnicity rather than the state.
Economic Supranationalism: Multiple countries joining together to achieve benefits in trade, commerce, and financial markets.
World Trade Organization (WTO): A global organization dealing with the rules of trade between nations to ensure fair and predictable trade flows.
Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries (OPEC): An international organization of oil-producing countries that coordinates and unifies petroleum policies.
Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN): A regional organization promoting economic growth, peace, and collaboration among Southeast Asian countries.
African Union (AU): A continental organization promoting economic development, peace, security, and unity among African states.
Arctic Council: A regional organization focused on sustainable development and environmental protection in the Arctic region.
European Union (EU): A political and economic union of European states, aiming for economic integration and political cooperation.
United Nations (UN): An international organization formed to increase cooperation, peace, and security among member states worldwide.
North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO): A military alliance of North American and European states committed to mutual defense.
United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA): A trade agreement that replaced NAFTA, aiming to promote free trade and economic cooperation in North America.
Census: An official count or survey of a population, typically recording details such as age, sex, occupation, and ethnicity.
