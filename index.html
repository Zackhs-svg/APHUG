Unit 1: Thinking Geographically

Absolute Location: The exact position of a place on the Earth's surface, often given in latitude and longitude.
Relative Location: The position of a place in relation to another place.
Map Projection: A method for representing the curved surface of the Earth on a flat map, with varying degrees of distortion.
Spatial Patterns: Describes where phenomena are located on Earth and how they are arranged, such as clustering or dispersal.
Clustering: A spatial pattern where phenomena are grouped closely together.
Dispersal: A spatial pattern where phenomena are spread apart with more space between them.
Elevation: The height of a geographical location relative to sea level, often depicted on isoline maps.
Scale: The relationship between distance on a map and the actual distance on Earth. Can be represented as a ratio or bar scale.
Large-Scale Map: A map that shows a smaller area with greater detail.
Small-Scale Map: A map that shows a larger area with less detail.
Choropleth Map: A thematic map that uses different colors to represent data density or values across regions.
Dot Distribution Map: A thematic map using dots to visualize the location or density of phenomena.
Graduated Symbol Map: A thematic map using symbols of varying sizes to indicate the magnitude of phenomena.
Isoline Map: A thematic map using lines to connect points of equal value, commonly used for elevation or weather data.
Cartogram: A thematic map that distorts the size of regions based on data values, such as population.
Mercator Projection: A map projection that preserves direction but distorts sizes, especially near the poles.
Peters Projection: A map projection that preserves area size but distorts shapes.
Robinson Projection: A compromise map projection that balances size and shape distortions across the map.
Geographic Information System (GIS): Software for analyzing and visualizing spatial data, used for research or decision-making.
Global Positioning System (GPS): A satellite-based system used to determine the precise location of an object or place on Earth.
Remote Sensing: The process of gathering data about Earth's surface from satellites or aerial imagery.

Unit 2: Population and Migration

Population Distribution: The pattern of where people live on Earth, influenced by physical factors like climate, landforms, and water bodies, and human factors like culture, economics, history, and politics.
Population Density: A measure of how many people occupy a given unit of land. Calculated using three methods: arithmetic, physiological, and agricultural density.
Arithmetic Density: The total population divided by the total land area, which includes uninhabitable areas.
Physiological Density: The total population divided by the amount of arable land, providing insight into the pressure on land to produce food.
Agricultural Density: The number of farmers divided by the amount of arable land, indicating levels of mechanization in agriculture.
Dependency Ratio: The ratio of dependents (under 15 and over 65) to the working-age population, showing the economic burden on the labor force.
Sex Ratio: The number of males compared to females in a population. Globally, it is approximately 101 males to 100 females.
Population Pyramid: A graphical representation of the age and sex structure of a population, used to interpret growth trends (e.g., rapid growth, stable, or decline).
Crude Birth Rate (CBR): The number of births per 1,000 people in a given year.
Total Fertility Rate (TFR): The average number of children a woman will have during her childbearing years, typically between ages 15 and 49.
Crude Death Rate (CDR): The number of deaths per 1,000 people in a given year.
Rate of Natural Increase (RNI): The difference between the crude birth rate and crude death rate, indicating population growth or decline without migration.
Doubling Time: The time it takes for a population to double in size, based on its growth rate.
Carrying Capacity: The maximum population an environment can sustain based on available resources, influenced by factors like technology and consumption.
Demographic Transition Model (DTM): A model showing population changes over time as a society industrializes, progressing through five stages from high birth and death rates to population decline.
Epidemiological Transition Model (ETM): A model explaining changes in death rates due to diseases, transitioning from infectious diseases (e.g., plague) to degenerative diseases (e.g., cancer).
Malthusian Theory: A theory predicting population growth would outpace food production, leading to famine, which was later disproved due to agricultural advancements.
Pro-Natalist Policies: Government policies encouraging higher birth rates, often through incentives like parental leave and child care support.
Anti-Natalist Policies: Government policies aimed at reducing birth rates, such as China's one-child policy.
Push Factors: Negative factors driving people to leave a location, such as war, famine, or persecution.
Pull Factors: Positive factors attracting people to a location, such as job opportunities or political stability.
Intervening Obstacles: Challenges migrants face when moving to a new location, such as political barriers or lack of resources.
Intervening Opportunities: Factors that encourage migrants to settle in a location along their journey instead of reaching their original destination.
Forced Migration: Migration compelled by external factors, such as slavery, war, or persecution.
Voluntary Migration: Migration based on a person's choice, often for economic or social reasons.
Arithmetic Density: The total number of people divided by the total land area.
Physiological Density: The number of people per unit area of arable land.
Agricultural Density: The number of farmers per unit area of farmland.
Demographic Transition Model: A model that describes population change over time through stages of birth and death rates.
Malthusian Theory: The theory that population growth will outpace agricultural production, leading to famine and conflict.
Carrying Capacity: The maximum number of individuals that an environment can sustainably support.
Dependency Ratio: The number of people under age 15 and over age 64 compared to the number of people active in the labor force.
Population Pyramid: A bar graph representing the distribution of population by age and sex.
Fertility Rate: The average number of children a woman will have during her childbearing years.
Mortality Rate: The number of deaths in a population per unit of time.

Unit 3: Cultural Patterns and Processes

Cultural Landscape: The visible imprint of human activity and culture on the landscape.
Cultural Diffusion: The spread of cultural beliefs and social activities from one group to another.
Ethnocentrism: Evaluating other cultures based on the standards of one’s own culture.
Language Family: A group of languages related through a common ancestral language.
Globalization: The process by which businesses or other organizations develop international influence or start operating on an international scale.
Ethnic Enclave: A geographic area with a high concentration of a particular ethnicity.
Universalizing Religion: A religion that attempts to appeal to all people regardless of location or culture.
Ethnic Religion: A religion that is closely tied to a specific ethnic group and does not seek converts.

Unit 4: Political Patterns and Processes

Sovereignty: The authority of a state to govern itself without outside interference.
Sovereignty: The authority of a state to govern itself without outside interference.
Nation-State: A state whose territory corresponds to a single ethnicity that has been transformed into a nationality.
Centrifugal Forces: Forces that divide a state, such as internal religious, linguistic, or ideological differences.
Centripetal Forces: Forces that unify a state, such as a shared sense of identity or a common culture.
Boundary Types: Includes geometric, physical, cultural, and superimposed boundaries.
Federal State: A system of government in which power is divided between a central authority and constituent units.
Unitary State: A state governed as a single entity with a central authority.
Devolution: The transfer of power from a central government to regional governments within a state.
Gerrymandering: The process of redrawing legislative boundaries to benefit the party in power.
